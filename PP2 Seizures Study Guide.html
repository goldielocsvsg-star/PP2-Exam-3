🧠 What is Cognition?

Cognition refers to all processes involved in thought: how one receives sensory input, processes it, stores it, retrieves it, and uses it. It exists on a continuum — intact or impaired — and can change over time.

6 Domains of Cognition
Perceptual Motor Function Language Learning & Memory Social Cognition Complex Attention Executive Function
🧬 Cognition Processing in the Brain
  • Cerebrum (frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital lobes) — where most cognitive tasks occur
  • Limbic System (overlaps cerebrum & diencephalon) — ability to reason, express personality, and interact with environmental stimuli
  • Hippocampus (within limbic system) — commits information to long-term memory
⚠️
Critical Exam ConceptCognitive impairment is NOT a normal part of aging. Neuronal atrophy occurs normally with age, but cognitive/intellectual loss is indicative of disease. Committing new information to memory becomes less efficient, but memory functioning itself remains intact in healthy aging.

🗺️ Big Picture — Conditions in This Module
Delirium (acute, hours–days, potentially reversible) → Dementia (chronic, progressive, irreversible) → Alzheimer's Disease (most common dementia; ↓ ACh) → Parkinson's Disease (dopamine deficiency in striatum; ↑ relative ACh)

Each condition has unique pathophysiology, unique clinical manifestations, and unique pharmacological treatment. Understanding the mechanism → imbalance → drug fix chain is the key to mastering this module.
Acute Confusional State
⚡ Delirium
Sudden-onset cognitive disturbance — always find and treat the underlying cause
📖 Definition

A state of disturbed consciousness and altered cognition with a rapid onset over hours or a few days. It is a common complication in the hospitalized elderly population and represents a medical emergency requiring prompt identification of the cause.

🔬 Pathophysiology
  • Disorder of cognitive function, consciousness, or perception
  • Disruption of widely distributed brain networks — not localized to a single area
  • Metabolic disturbances interfere with neuronal metabolism or synaptic transmission
  • Neurotransmitter imbalance: ↓ Acetylcholine + ↑ Excess Dopamine
  • Drugs and toxins may interfere with neurotransmission at the synapse
🩺 Conditions That Cause Delirium
Substance-Related
  • Drug intoxication
  • Alcohol or drug withdrawal
  • Post-anesthesia
Key Medication Risk Factors
  • Narcotics
  • Benzodiazepines
  • Anticholinergics
Metabolic / Systemic
  • Hypoglycemia / thyroid storm
  • Electrolyte imbalances
  • Dehydration
  • Hypoxia
  • Heart, kidney, or liver failure
  • F&E imbalances
Structural / Infectious
  • Brain trauma, surgery, tumors
  • Meningoencephalitis
  • Sepsis / systemic infection
  • Fever (febrile state)
High-Risk Settings
  • ICU patients
  • Post-operative patients
  • CNS depressant withdrawal
  • Elderly hospitalized patients
⏱️
TimelineDelirium develops over 2–3 days with acute disturbance in attention and awareness (ANS overactivity). Onset may also be sudden.
🔴 Clinical Manifestations
Cognitive & Perceptual
  • Dulled awareness of environment
  • Reduced ability to focus, sustain, shift attention
  • Memory and judgment impaired
  • Disorientation
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Hallucinations
  • Delusions
  • Vivid dreams / nightmares
  • Altered perceptions
Behavioral & Physical
  • Rapid, rambling, incoherent speech
  • Intense emotional swings
  • Restlessness / irritability
  • Tremulousness / tremors / restless movements
  • Insomnia / poor appetite
  • Seizures
  • Flushed skin, dilated pupils
  • Tachycardia, fever, diaphoresis
  • Incoherent conversation / distress
💊 Treatment Approach

Establish the underlying cause — all treatment is directed at correcting the primary disorder. Delirium is potentially reversible when the trigger is identified and treated promptly.

Progressive Neurodegenerative Syndrome
🧩 Dementia
Chronic, irreversible deterioration of cerebral function — slow onset, progressive decline
📖 Definition & Core Pathophysiology

Dementia is a deterioration and progressive failure of many cerebral functions with impairment of intellectual processes including a decrease in orientation, memory, language, judgment, and decision-making. Behavioral alterations (agitation, wandering, aggression) also occur.

Underlying Mechanisms
  • Neuron degeneration
  • Compression of brain tissue
  • Atherosclerosis of cerebral vessels
  • Brain trauma or infection
  • Neuroinflammation
  • Genetic predisposition
Viral Causes
  • HIV — nerve cell degeneration and brain atrophy
  • Slow-growing viruses — associated with Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease → nerve cell degeneration and brain atrophy
📚 Types of Dementia — Know Them All
Most Common
Alzheimer's Disease Amyloid plaques + neurofibrillary tau tangles; ↓ acetylcholine; chromosome 19 or 21 association
Prion Disease
Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease Slow-growing virus; rapid progression; associated with prion proteins
Protein Deposits
Dementia with Lewy Body Alpha-synuclein protein deposits; overlaps with Parkinson's Disease features
Frontal/Temporal
Frontotemporal Dementia Personality and behavior changes predominate early; frontal lobe atrophy
Genetic
Huntington Disease Autosomal dominant; progressive striatum degeneration; involuntary movements
Vascular
Vascular Dementia Multiple strokes or reduced cerebral blood flow; step-wise deterioration
🔑
Key Clinical Feature: Slow Onset + Irreversible Dementia has a slow onset and is irreversible — this is the critical distinction from delirium. Clinical manifestations for specific dementia types (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's) are covered in their respective tabs.
Most Common Dementia
🔬 Alzheimer's Disease
Progressive amyloid/tau-driven neurodegeneration with acetylcholine deficiency
🧬 Pathophysiology — The "What & Why"

The cause of AD is unknown, but genetic associations with chromosomes 19 or 21 have been identified.

🔴 Hallmark #1 — Neuritic Plaques
  • Extracellular accumulation of amyloid beta protein
  • Located in cerebral cortex & hippocampus (memory region)
  • Disrupt nerve impulse transmission → cause neuron death
🔴 Hallmark #2 — Neurofibrillary Tangles
  • Intraneuronal tangles of tau protein
  • Destabilize the neuron's internal skeleton
  • Contribute to progressive cell death

Why ACh is Central to TreatmentLoss of synapses and acetylcholine (ACh) is responsible for the decline in memory, attention, and cognitive function. This is the pharmacological rationale for cholinesterase inhibitors — they preserve the remaining ACh by blocking the enzyme that degrades it.
📊 Five-Stage Progression of Alzheimer's
Stage 1
Mild Cognitive Impairment
Cognitive: Mild memory loss
Functional: Possibly depression (vs. apathy); mild anxiety. May still live independently.
Stage 2
Early Stage
Cognitive: Measurable short-term memory loss; difficulty word-finding; cognition changes compared to previous behavior
Functional: Mild IADL problems
Stage 3
Middle Stage
Cognitive: Moderate–severe; impaired reasoning/judgment/problem solving; disorientation to time, place, and person; progressive memory loss
Functional: IADL-dependent; some ADL problems
Stage 4
Late Stage
Cognitive: Little cognitive ability; language not clear
Functional: ADL-dependent; incontinent
Stage 5
End Stage
Cognitive: No significant function; loss of orientation to self
Functional: Non-ambulatory / bedbound; cannot sense hunger or thirst; difficulty swallowing
🔴 Clinical Manifestations
  • Progressive memory deficits → loss of cognitive and executive function
  • Forgetfulness (recent events first)
  • Disoriented and confused
  • Inability to concentrate
  • Poor judgment
  • Difficulty with problem-solving, mathematics, language
  • Emotional upset
  • Mental status changes
  • Mood changes (depression, agitation)
  • Motor changes related to posterior frontal lobe involvement
  • Weight loss
  • Eventual loss of all ADL ability
  • Brain atrophy: widening sulci, shrinking gyri, enlarged ventricles, hippocampal shrinkage
⭐ High-Yield Exam Q — Neurotransmitter Deficit in AD
Acetylcholine (ACh) is the neurotransmitter that appears insufficient in clients with Alzheimer's disease. Loss of synapses and ACh in the cholinergic neuropathways contributes to the decline in memory, attention, and cognitive function. This is why cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine) are the first-line pharmacological treatment.
Dopamine-Deficient Neurodegenerative Disorder
🔄 Parkinson's Disease
Too little striatal dopamine + too much acetylcholine = motor dysfunction
🔬 Pathophysiology — The Core Problem

Parkinson's Disease is a slowly progressive neurodegenerative disorder — non-curative. Motor symptoms result from the loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra, which reduce dopamine levels in the striatum.

⚖️
The Fundamental Imbalance — This is EVERYTHING for PD Normal brain = Balance between Dopamine (inhibitory) and Acetylcholine (stimulatory) in basal ganglia cells.
PD brain = ↓ Dopamine in striatum/substantia nigra → ↑ Relative Acetylcholine dominance → motor dysfunction (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, postural instability).
ALL PD medications aim to RESTORE this dopamine/ACh balance.
🚶 Motor Symptoms

Caused by loss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra:

Resting Tremor Bradykinesia Rigidity Postural Instability Gait Disturbance Loss of Muscle Movement
💊
Drug Benefit TargetDrug benefits improve bradykinesia, gait disturbance, and postural instability. The overarching goal is to improve the patient's ability to perform ADLs.
🧠 Non-Motor & Early Symptoms
ANS Disorders
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Swallowing and GI disorders
  • Bowel & bladder abnormalities
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Orthostatic hypotension
Early Symptoms (Pre-motor)
  • Loss of smell (anosmia)
  • Excessive salivation
  • Worsening handwriting (micrographia)
  • Tremor, slower gait, reduced voice volume
⚖️ Pharmacological Strategy — Two Approaches
⬆️
Type 1 Drugs — Increase Dopamine (A) Increase dopamine concentration: levodopa, rasagiline
(B) Increase dopamine release: amantadine
(C) Stimulate dopamine receptors directly: pramipexole, ropinirole
⬇️
Type 2 Drugs — Decrease ACh Activity Anticholinergic drugs block stimulant cholinergic effects:
benztropine, diphenhydramine, trihexyphenidyl
💡 Conceptual Memory Aid — Carbidopa/Levodopa
"Levodopa hitches a ride in CARbidopa's car across the BBB"
Levodopa cannot cross the blood-brain barrier alone. Carbidopa acts as the carrier that escorts levodopa across the BBB. Only after crossing does levodopa convert to dopamine and exert its therapeutic effect. Without carbidopa, levodopa has no CNS effect.
Pharmacology
💊 Alzheimer's Disease Medications
Goal: Slow the loss of memory and cognition — NOT a cure. Improve quality of life and ADL performance.
🎯
Treatment Selection by Disease Severity Mild to Moderate AD → Cholinesterase Inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine)
Moderate to Severe AD → NMDA Receptor Antagonist (memantine)
Combined → Memantine can be combined with donepezil for advanced disease

All medications: No cure — slow progression only. Expected outcomes: improved cognitive function, behavioral symptoms, quality of life, and ADL ability.

🔵 Class: Cholinesterase Inhibitors (Mild to Moderate AD)

⚙️ Class Mechanism of Action
These drugs inhibit acetylcholinesterase — the enzyme that breaks down ACh in the synaptic cleft. More ACh remains available → enhances transmission by neurons not yet destroyed by AD. They do NOT stop the disease; they slow cognitive decline by preserving whatever ACh remains.

Side effects = CHOLINERGIC / PARASYMPATHETIC stimulation: bradycardia (fall risk), bronchoconstriction (caution with asthma/COPD), nausea, diarrhea, vomiting.
Donepezil
Cholinesterase Inhibitor · Mild to Severe Alzheimer's Disease
Prototype Drug
🎯 Mechanism of Action
Inhibits acetylcholinesterase → ↑ ACh at cholinergic synapses → enhances transmission by cholinergic neurons not yet destroyed by AD. Does not reverse damage — only supports surviving neurons.
💊 Therapeutic Use
Mild to moderate AD per lecture; mild to severe AD per textbook. Slows cognitive decline. Not a cure. Modestly improves cognition, behavior, and function.
⚠️ Adverse Effects (Cholinergic/Parasympathetic)
Bradycardia → Fall Risk! Nausea / Vomiting Diarrhea Bronchoconstriction Dizziness / Headache
🚫 Drug–Drug Interactions
Anticholinergics → ↓ efficacy (opposing mechanism) Antihistamines → ↓ efficacy Antipsychotics → ↓ efficacy NSAIDs → ↑ GI bleeding risk Cholinesterase inhibitors → ↑ toxicity (synergistic)
🩺 Nursing Considerations
  • Titrate slowly to desired dose
  • Administer at bedtime (HS)
  • Swallow tablets whole
  • Dissolve ODT (oral disintegrating tablet) on tongue, follow with water
  • Monitor heart rate; report dizziness or falls
  • Use cautiously with heart disease
  • Use cautiously with asthma/COPD (bronchoconstriction risk)
✅ Expected Outcomes
No cure. Slows progression. Modest improvement in: cognition, behavior, function, quality of life, and ADL performance. Slight delay in disease progression.
Rivastigmine
Cholinesterase Inhibitor · Mild to Moderate Alzheimer's Disease
Oral or Transdermal Patch
⚠️ Adverse Effects
Same as donepezil, plus weight loss. Also: GI effects and bradycardia-related dizziness/falls.
🩺 Administration — Two Routes
  • Oral: Twice daily (BID) with meals
  • Transdermal Patch: Apply q24h to chest, upper arm, or lower back after removing previous patch
  • Do NOT repeat application site for 14 days
  • Monitor for dizziness, falls, low pulse, GI intolerance
⭐
Two Exam-Worthy Clinical Scenarios 1. Rivastigmine overdose → expect to give Atropine (anticholinergic antidote for cholinergic toxicity: nausea, diaphoresis, bronchoconstriction, bradycardia)
2. Rivastigmine + ibuprofen (NSAID) → monitor for GI bleeding
Galantamine
Cholinesterase Inhibitor · Mild to Moderate Alzheimer's Disease
Oral BID
⚠️ Adverse Effects
Same as donepezil, plus weight loss.
🩺 Administration
  • Oral BID — swallow whole
  • Monitor for cholinergic AEs, dizziness, falls, low pulse, GI intolerance

🟣 Class: NMDA Receptor Antagonist (Moderate to Severe AD)

⚙️ Class Mechanism of Action
In AD, excess glutamate pathologically overstimulates NMDA receptors → excessive calcium (Ca²⁺) influx → neuronal damage and death. Memantine blocks NMDA receptors → restricts excessive Ca²⁺ entry → protects neurons. Normal physiological receptor activation can still occur (allows learning/memory signals to proceed normally).
Memantine
NMDA Receptor Antagonist · Moderate to Severe Alzheimer's Disease
Moderate–Severe AD
🎯 Mechanism
Modulates effects of glutamate at NMDA receptors. The receptor regulates calcium entry into neurons. Binding of glutamate to the receptor promotes Ca²⁺ influx, signaling learning and memory processes. In AD, excess glutamate = pathological persistent activation → excessive Ca²⁺ → cell damage. Memantine blocks this excess without preventing normal activation.
⚠️ Adverse Effects
Generally well tolerated. 5–7% incidence of:
Dizziness Headache Confusion
🩺 Nursing Considerations
  • Swallow whole, OR sprinkle capsule on soft food, OR give as oral elixir (flexible administration for AD patients)
  • Can be combined with donepezil
  • Drug–Drug: other NMDA antagonists → synergistic effects
  • Monitor cognitive function and ADL ability over time
  • Teach patient/family that benefit is limited and variable
✅ Expected Outcomes
No cure; slows progression. May help maintain function a few months longer. May improve memory and thinking in some patients. Improves cognitive function, behavioral symptoms, quality of life, and ADLs.

Carbidopa / Levodopa
Dopaminergic Agent — Dopamine Replacement · Severe Parkinson's Disease
1st Line — Severe PD
🚗
The Key Concept — Why Carbidopa is Essential Levodopa cannot cross the blood-brain barrier alone. Carbidopa serves as the carrier that helps levodopa cross the BBB. Once inside the brain, levodopa is converted into dopamine, which then restores the dopamine/ACh balance in the striatum. Without carbidopa, levodopa is therapeutically useless.
⚠️ Adverse Effects
Nausea / Vomiting Dyskinesia (involuntary movements) Hypotension Dysthymias / Depression Anxiety, Agitation to Memory Impairment Cognitive Impairment Impulse Control Disorder Psychosis Darkened sweat / urine (harmless)
🚫 Drug–Drug Interactions
  • Antipsychotics (chlorpromazine, haloperidol) → ↓ levodopa effects
  • MAO Inhibitors → Toxicity = Hypertensive Crisis!
  • Anticholinergic agents → Can enhance responses to levodopa (excessive cholinergic stimulation = ↑ dyskinesias)
🩺 Nursing Considerations & Teaching
  • High-protein foods compete with levodopa for intestinal absorption → reduce therapeutic effect; space away from high-protein meals
  • Drug takes several months to work fully — set realistic expectations
  • If dyskinesia develops: lower dose, add amantadine, or consider surgery / electrical stimulation
  • Warn about "On-Off" episodes — unpredictable times when levodopa suddenly stops working → symptoms return even when drug level should be adequate; can last minutes to hours
  • During "Off Times," drugs like pramipexole or ropinirole can help
  • Also available as 3-drug combination: levodopa/carbidopa/entacapone (Stalevo)
✅ Expected Outcomes
Not a cure. Goal: improved ability to carry out ADLs. Benefits improve bradykinesia, gait disturbance, and postural instability.
Pramipexole & Ropinirole
Dopamine Agonists — Direct Receptor Activation · Mild–Moderate PD
1st Line (if not levodopa) or Adjunct
🎯 Mechanism
Bind selectively to dopamine-2 (D2) and dopamine-3 (D3) receptor subtypes. Directly activate dopamine receptors in the striatum. Do NOT need carbidopa — do not need another drug to cross the BBB.
✅ Advantages Over Levodopa
  • No companion drug needed to cross BBB
  • Lower incidence of response failures
  • Lower incidence of dyskinesia
  • Also used for restless leg syndrome (RLS)
⚠️ Adverse Effects
Hallucinations Daytime Sleepiness Compulsive Behaviors (r/t ↑ dopamine) Postural Hypotension/Dizziness Nausea Constipation
🩺 Nursing Considerations
Benefits take several weeks to develop — educate patient. Used for mild-to-moderate symptoms or as supplement to levodopa.
Entacapone
COMT Inhibitor (Catechol-O-Methyltransferase Inhibitor) · Adjunct to Levodopa
Adjunct — "Wearing Off"
🎯 Mechanism
Inhibits metabolism of levodopa in the intestine and peripheral tissues by blocking the enzyme COMT → prolongs levodopa availability in the brain. Beneficial for patients experiencing the "wearing off" effect of levodopa/carbidopa.
⚠️ Adverse Effects
Dyskinesias Sleep Disturbances Impulse Control Disorder Orthostatic Hypotension / Dizziness
Selegiline
MAO-B Inhibitor (Monoamine Oxidase B Inhibitor) · Early Treatment
Early PD / Adjunct
🎯 Mechanism
Inhibits breakdown of dopamine by MAO-B → more dopamine remains available in the synaptic cleft. May be combined with levodopa to reduce the "wearing-off" effect.
⚠️ Adverse Effects
Insomnia (CNS excitation) Orthostatic Hypotension Dizziness GI Symptoms
🚫 Dangerous Drug Interactions — MANY!
MAOIs (depression) → HTN Crisis! Opioids → Respiratory Depression! SSRIs → Serotonin Syndrome!
🩺 Nursing Considerations & Teaching
  • Administer before noon — insomnia is related to CNS excitation; giving later worsens sleep
  • At high doses: avoid tyramine-containing foods — especially aged, cured, or fermented foods (cheese, wine, salami)
Amantadine
Dopamine Releaser (also NMDA Receptor Antagonist) · Reduces Dyskinesias
Dyskinesia Control
🎯 Mechanism
Promotes release of dopamine from neurons AND blocks dopamine reuptake → more dopamine available in the synaptic cleft. Also has NMDA receptor antagonist activity (decreases tremors). Primarily used to reduce dyskinesias caused by levodopa.
⚠️ Adverse Effects
CNS Effects
Confusion Lightheadedness Anxiety
Anticholinergic Effects
Blurred Vision Urinary Retention Dry Mouth

🟤 Class: Anticholinergics — For Tremors / EPS

⚙️ Class Mechanism
Inhibit the actions of acetylcholine in the brain → block ACh receptors → reduce excessive cholinergic activity that dominates when dopamine is depleted → improve dopamine/ACh balance.

Classic anticholinergic side effect mnemonic: "Can't pee, can't see, can't spit, can't sh*t"
= Urinary retention, blurred vision, dry mouth, constipation
Benztropine
Anticholinergic Agent · 2nd Line Treatment for Tremors / EPS
2nd Line Tremors / EPS
🎯 Mechanism & Use
Selectively blocks central cholinergic receptors → balances dopaminergic activity → reduces severity of tremors and rigidity. Used for PD tremors (2nd line) and drug-induced extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS). Routes: PO, IM, or IV (IM/IV used only for dystonia).
⚠️ Adverse Effects (Anticholinergic)
Dry Mouth Blurred Vision Photophobia Urinary Retention Constipation Tachycardia Drowsiness Decreased Sweating
👵
BEERS CRITERIA — Geriatric Safety Alert! Anticholinergic drugs have been designated as potentially inappropriate for use in geriatric patients.
Assess elderly clients for: confusion, disorientation, agitation, and psychotic-like symptoms.
Also use caution (or avoid) in patients with glaucoma (anticholinergics increase intraocular pressure).
Diphenhydramine
Antihistamine with Central Anticholinergic Activity · Acute EPS / Dystonia
Short-Term / Acute Use
🎯 Mechanism
Blocks histamine H1 receptors AND has central anticholinergic activity. Because it penetrates the CNS, it provides anticholinergic effects → can reduce tremor or help resolve acute dystonic reactions / EPS. More commonly a short-term option — not a core long-term PD drug.
⚠️ Adverse Effects
Drowsiness / Sedation Dizziness Dry Mouth Blurred Vision Confusion (esp. elderly) Urinary Retention Constipation
⚠️
Caution in Older Adults & Cognitive Patients Can worsen confusion and cognition in older adults or those with memory symptoms. Additive CNS/sedative effects with alcohol or other CNS depressants. Use caution with glaucoma or urinary retention/prostatic hypertrophy.

🔑 Alzheimer's Must-Knows
  • Patho: Amyloid plaques + tau tangles → neuron death → ↓ ACh → brain atrophy (widening sulci, shrinking gyri)
  • Location: Cerebral cortex + hippocampus
  • Key genetic link: Chromosomes 19 or 21
  • Mild–Moderate: Cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine)
  • Moderate–Severe: Memantine (NMDA receptor antagonist)
  • Can combine: Memantine + donepezil
  • Goal: Slow progression — NOT a cure
  • Donepezil: Give at HS; anticholinergics/antihistamines/antipsychotics reduce efficacy
  • Rivastigmine OD → Give atropine
  • ChEI + NSAIDs → ↑ GI bleeding risk
  • Rivastigmine patch: No site repeat for 14 days
🔑 Parkinson's Must-Knows
  • Patho: ↓ Dopamine in substantia nigra/striatum → ↑ relative ACh dominance → motor dysfunction
  • Cardinal motor sx: Tremor, bradykinesia, rigidity, postural instability
  • Levodopa alone = useless — needs carbidopa to cross BBB
  • High-protein foods: Compete with levodopa absorption → space from meals
  • MAOIs + levodopa = HTN crisis!
  • Selegiline → give BEFORE NOON (insomnia); SSRIs = serotonin syndrome; opioids = resp. depression
  • Amantadine: Reduces levodopa-induced dyskinesias + NMDA activity
  • "On-Off" effect: Levodopa suddenly stops working; symptoms return unpredictably
  • Benztropine — Beers Criteria: Caution/avoid in elderly; CI with glaucoma
  • Pramipexole/ropinirole: Also for restless leg syndrome; benefits take weeks
🧠 Master Comparison — Delirium vs Dementia vs Alzheimer's vs Parkinson's
ACUTE → CHRONIC → ACh ↓ → Dopamine ↓
Delirium = ACUTE (hours–days) · Reversible · Disturbed consciousness · ↓ ACh + ↑ Dopamine
Dementia = CHRONIC (months–years) · Irreversible · Slow onset · Multiple types
Alzheimer's = Most common dementia · Amyloid/tau · ↓ ACh · Fix: increase ACh (ChEIs) or protect neurons (memantine)
Parkinson's = ↓ Dopamine in striatum · ↑ relative ACh · Fix: restore dopamine or block excess ACh

Delirium: Find the cause and fix it. Dementia/AD/PD: Slow progression, manage symptoms — no cure.
