Cognition

Delirium · Alzheimer's Disease · Parkinson's Disease

Definition of Cognition Obj 1

The mental action or process of acquiring knowledge and understanding through thought, experience, and the senses. Involves memory, attention, executive function, language, and perception.

AD Pathophysiology Obj 3
  • Acetylcholine (ACh) is markedly decreased — primary deficit
  • Beta-amyloid plaques accumulate outside neurons
  • Neurofibrillary tangles (tau protein) inside neurons
  • Progressive cortical atrophy, especially hippocampus
  • Memory loss begins with short-term → long-term
  • Aphasia, agnosia, apraxia develop in later stages
Risk Factors Summary Obj 2
Delirium Risk Factors
  • UTI / infections (most common in elderly)
  • Polypharmacy / drug toxicity
  • Electrolyte imbalances
  • Sleep deprivation / ICU environment
  • Advanced age, dementia, sensory impairment
  • Urinary retention / catheter insertion
AD Risk Factors
  • Age >65 (greatest risk factor)
  • ApoE-ε4 allele (genetic)
  • Head trauma history
  • Female sex (longer lifespan)
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Low education / cognitive reserve
🚨 EXAM ALERT — Delirium vs Dementia
The #1 tested differentiator: Onset. Delirium = ACUTE/sudden. Dementia = GRADUAL/chronic. A patient who "suddenly seems confused" has delirium until proven otherwise. Always look for an underlying cause (UTI, medication, infection).

Cognition Pharmacology

Alzheimer's & Parkinson's Drug Therapy · Obj 4, 5, 6

Cholinesterase Inhibitors — Alzheimer's Disease Mechanism: ↑ ACh
Mechanism of Action
Inhibit acetylcholinesterase (AChE), the enzyme that breaks down ACh → increases ACh levels in synaptic cleft → improves cholinergic neurotransmission. Does NOT cure; slows functional decline.
Donepezil
Cholinesterase Inhibitor · 2nd & 3rd Gen
Route / Dosing
Oral (tablet or ODT); taken at bedtime (causes insomnia if AM). QD dosing.
Side Effects
⚠ Bradycardia Nausea Diarrhea Insomnia Muscle cramps
Nursing / Evaluation
Monitor heart rate (risk of AV block/bradycardia). Evaluate ADL performance as therapeutic outcome. Drug-drug: NSAIDs ↑ GI bleed risk; anticholinergics counteract effects.
Donepezil + Memantine
May be combined in moderate-severe AD. Memantine = NMDA antagonist; different mechanism (protects from glutamate excitotoxicity). Evaluate: functional status, memory tasks, ADLs.
Rivastigmine
Cholinesterase Inhibitor — Transdermal Patch
Route / Administration
✓ Transdermal PATCH (preferred)
Also available oral capsule. Patch provides steady plasma levels, fewer GI side effects.
📌 Patient Teaching — PATCH
Rotate patch sites daily. Do NOT apply new patch to same site for 14 days. Apply to upper arm, upper back, or chest. Remove old patch before new one. Dispose carefully (still contains drug).
Side Effects / Contraindications
Nausea, vomiting, anorexia Skin irritation at patch site
Contraindicated: Known hypersensitivity to carbamate derivatives.
Galantamine
Cholinesterase Inhibitor + Nicotinic Receptor Modulator
Unique Feature
Also modulates nicotinic receptors (dual mechanism). Available as extended-release capsule.
⛔ Contraindications
🚫 Severe hepatic impairment 🚫 Severe renal impairment
(renally cleared; adjust dose for mild-moderate impairment)
Side Effects
Nausea, vomiting Diarrhea Weight loss Anorexia
⚠ Class-Wide Side Effects — All ChEIs
All cholinesterase inhibitors share cholinergic side effects (SLUDGE overlap): Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, bradycardia, bronchoconstriction, increased secretions, urinary incontinence. Anticholinergic drugs COUNTERACT these medications — avoid concurrent use!
Parkinson's Disease Pharmacology Goal: ↑ Dopamine OR ↓ ACh
Levodopa / Carbidopa (Sinemet)
Dopamine Precursor + Decarboxylase Inhibitor · GOLD STANDARD
Mechanism of Action
Levodopa crosses the BBB → converted to dopamine in the CNS.
Carbidopa blocks peripheral decarboxylase → prevents conversion BEFORE crossing BBB → more levodopa reaches brain → reduces peripheral side effects.
Side Effects
Dark urine/sweat (harmless!) Orthostatic hypotension Dyskinesia (long-term) Nausea On-off phenomenon Hallucinations
📌 Patient Teaching
• Avoid high-protein meals — amino acids compete with levodopa for transport across BBB → ↓ drug absorption
• Change positions slowly (orthostatic hypotension)
• Do NOT abruptly stop (risk of neuroleptic malignant syndrome–like reaction)
• Dark sweat/urine is HARMLESS — reassure patient
Evaluation of Effectiveness
Improved motor control, reduced tremors, improved mobility, ability to perform ADLs, reduced bradykinesia.
Pramipexole (Mirapex)
Dopamine Agonist (D2/D3)
Action
Directly stimulates dopamine receptors. Used alone or with levodopa. Also used for restless leg syndrome.
⚠ KEY Side Effects
😴 Sudden "sleep attacks" 🎰 Compulsive behaviors Orthostatic hypotension Hallucinations

Nursing: Warn patients to avoid driving; screen for gambling, hypersexuality, binge eating behaviors.
Selegiline (Eldepryl)
MAO-B Inhibitor
Mechanism
Selectively inhibits MAO-B enzyme → prevents breakdown of dopamine → more dopamine available in synapse. Used as adjunct to levodopa.
⚠ Teaching / Contraindications
🚫 Tyramine-rich foods → hypertensive crisis
Avoid: aged cheese, cured meats, wine, soy sauce.
🚫 Do NOT combine with SSRIs/meperidine → serotonin syndrome
🚫 Contraindicated with meperidine (Demerol)
Benztropine (Cogentin)
Anticholinergic (Muscarinic Blocker)
Mechanism / Use
Blocks ACh to restore dopamine-ACh balance in the striatum. Primarily for tremors & rigidity. Also used for drug-induced EPS (antipsychotics).
Side Effects (ANTICHOLINERGIC)
Dry mouth Blurred vision Urinary retention Constipation Confusion Tachycardia
Evaluation
Reduction in tremors. Monitor for urinary retention (bladder scan, I&O), confusion in elderly. AVOID in elderly (Beers Criteria).
Amantadine
Antiviral / Dopaminergic Agent
Mechanism
Stimulates dopamine release from nerve endings + mild NMDA antagonism + anticholinergic properties. Used for dyskinesia and early PD.
Teaching / Side Effects
Livedo reticularis (mottled skin) Ankle edema CNS effects: confusion, hallucinations Do NOT stop abruptly → rebound symptoms

Intracranial Regulation

ICP/TBI · Seizures/Epilepsy · Hemorrhagic Stroke

Increased ICP & TBI — Monro-Kellie Hypothesis Obj 1, 2
Monro-Kellie Doctrine
The cranial vault has a fixed volume. The total volume of brain + CSF + blood = constant. If one component ↑, the others must ↓ to compensate. When compensation fails → ↑ ICP.

Normal ICP
Normal ICP = 5–15 mmHg. ICP >20 mmHg is abnormal. CPP (Cerebral Perfusion Pressure) = MAP − ICP. Target CPP: 60–70 mmHg.
Early Signs of ↑ ICP
  • Change in LOC — earliest and most sensitive sign
  • Headache (worse in morning)
  • Projectile vomiting (no nausea)
  • Visual changes (papilledema)
  • Behavioral changes / restlessness
Cushing's Triad (LATE — CRITICAL)
  • 🩺 Widened pulse pressure (↑ systolic, ↓ diastolic)
  • ❤️ Bradycardia
  • 🫁 Irregular respirations
  • ⚠ Cushing's Triad = Impending herniation — EMERGENCY

Seizure Disorders — Pathophysiology Obj 1, 2, 3
Etiology
Abnormal, excessive, synchronous electrical discharges from neurons. Can be focal (one area) or generalized. Causes: head trauma, brain tumor, infection (meningitis), electrolyte imbalances, stroke, febrile illness.
During Seizure — Safety Priority
  • Do NOT restrain patient
  • Turn patient to side-lying position (prevent aspiration)
  • Padded side rails up
  • Suction at bedside
  • Time the seizure
  • Protect head from injury
  • Do NOT put anything in the mouth
  • Oxygen delivery PRN
Status Epilepticus — EMERGENCY
  • Seizure lasting >5 min OR back-to-back seizures
  • Step 1: IV Benzodiazepine (lorazepam, diazepam) — immediate
  • Step 2: IV Phenytoin / fosphenytoin — long-acting
  • Step 3: General anesthesia if refractory
  • Protect airway — high aspiration risk
  • Monitor: O₂ sat, glucose, electrolytes
Hemorrhagic Stroke Obj 1, 2
Etiology / Pathophysiology
Rupture of a blood vessel → bleeding INTO brain tissue (intracerebral) or subarachnoid space. Causes: hypertension (most common), AVM, aneurysm, coagulopathy. Blood exerts direct pressure → ↑ ICP → ischemia to surrounding tissue.
Diagnosis
  • Non-contrast CT scan — GOLD STANDARD (immediate)
  • Blood appears as hyperdense (white) on CT
  • Done BEFORE any treatment
  • MRI for detailed view (slower)
  • LP if subarachnoid suspected and CT negative
⛔ Strict Contraindications
  • tPA (alteplase) — CONTRAINDICATED (will worsen bleed)
  • Anticoagulants — CONTRAINDICATED (heparin, warfarin)
  • Antiplatelets — CONTRAINDICATED
  • These are for ISCHEMIC stroke only!
🧠 Ischemic vs Hemorrhagic — The Key Distinction
Ischemic → blocked vessel → tPA CAN be used (within 4.5 hr, no contraindications)
Hemorrhagic → bleeding vessel → tPA ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED
ALWAYS get CT FIRST to differentiate! You cannot clinically distinguish them.

Intracranial Pharmacology

Antiepileptics · ICP Drugs · Obj 3, 4, 5

Mannitol (Osmitrol) Osmotic Diuretic
Mechanism
Osmotic diuretic → increases serum osmolality → draws water from brain tissue into vascular space → reduces cerebral edema and ↓ ICP.
Route
IV infusion (use filter needle — crystals can form). Given as bolus or drip.
Evaluation — Monitor
✓ Urine output (expect ↑ diuresis)
✓ Serum osmolality (goal 310–320)
✓ ICP improvement / neurological status
⚠ Electrolytes (risk of hyponatremia, hypokalemia)
⚠ Renal function (BUN/Creatinine)
Phenytoin (Dilantin)
Antiepileptic · Sodium Channel Blocker
Mechanism
Blocks voltage-gated sodium channels → stabilizes neuronal membranes → reduces rapid firing. Effective for tonic-clonic and focal seizures.
📌 Teaching
• Good oral hygiene — prevents gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth)
• Take with food (GI upset)
• Do NOT crush/chew extended-release
• Avoid abrupt discontinuation
• Report rash, ataxia, nystagmus
Side Effects
Nystagmus Ataxia Gingival hyperplasia Cardiovascular toxicity (IV too fast) Hirsutism Hepatotoxicity
Valproic Acid (Depakote)
Broad-Spectrum Antiepileptic
Mechanism
Increases GABA, blocks sodium and calcium channels. Broad spectrum — generalized and focal seizures, also used for migraines and bipolar.
⚠ Key Monitoring
🚨 Hepatotoxicity — monitor LFTs 🚨 Pancreatitis — monitor lipase/amylase 🚫 Teratogenic — Neural tube defects (Category D) Thrombocytopenia Weight gain Hair loss
Lamotrigine (Lamictal)
Antiepileptic · Sodium Channel Blocker
⚠ CRITICAL Side Effect
🚨 Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)
Life-threatening skin rash. Educate patient to IMMEDIATELY REPORT any rash. Titrate dose slowly to reduce risk (especially with valproic acid).
Other Side Effects
Dizziness Diplopia Headache Nausea
Gabapentin (Neurontin)
Antiepileptic / Neuropathic Pain
Teaching / Side Effects
Drowsiness, dizziness Ataxia Avoid alcohol (↑ CNS depression)
Do not drive until effects known. Do not abruptly stop. Renally excreted — dose adjust in renal impairment.
Carbamazepine (Tegretol)
Antiepileptic · Sodium Channel Blocker
Key Concerns
🚨 Agranulocytosis — monitor CBC 🚨 Aplastic anemia SJS risk (especially HLA-B*1502 in Asian populations) Induces CYP450 — many drug interactions Hyponatremia (SIADH-like)
Pregabalin (Lyrica)
Antiepileptic / Neuropathic Pain
Side Effects
Dizziness, drowsiness Weight gain Peripheral edema Blurred vision Schedule V controlled substance — abuse potential.
Topiramate (Topamax)
Broad-Spectrum Antiepileptic
Teaching / Side Effects
🚨 Kidney stones — drink plenty of fluids! Cognitive dulling ("Dopamax") Word-finding difficulties Weight loss Angle-closure glaucoma Metabolic acidosis

Case Studies

Blueprint-focused scenarios with clinical priorities

🧩 Parkinson's Disease — Complex Patient Blueprint Priority
Scenario
A patient with Parkinson's disease on Carbidopa-Levodopa and Benztropine is presenting with confusion, elevated blood glucose, and decreased urine output. You need to differentiate symptoms and manage complications.
PD Signs & Symptoms to Recognize
  • Resting tremor (pill-rolling)
  • Cogwheel rigidity
  • Bradykinesia / akinesia
  • Shuffling gait, festination
  • Postural instability, falls risk
  • Masked facies (flat expression)
  • Micrographia
  • Dysarthria, dysphagia
  • Non-motor: depression, constipation, autonomic dysfunction
Differentiating PD vs AD vs Delirium
  • PD: Motor symptoms + LOC preserved
  • AD: Memory loss, aphasia, executive dysfunction
  • Delirium: ACUTE onset, fluctuating LOC, reversible cause
  • A PD patient can develop delirium (new acute confusion) AND can have dementia (Lewy body)
  • Key: Is the confusion NEW and ACUTE? → Think delirium first
Complications + Medications to Manage
ComplicationCause in PDLabs to MonitorManagement
Constipation Anticholinergic drugs (benztropine), decreased mobility, PD autonomic dysfunction No specific lab; clinical assessment Bulk-forming laxatives, hydration, dietary fiber, stool softeners; reduce anticholinergic load
Dehydration Dysphagia → decreased PO intake, diaphoresis, constipation ↑ BUN, ↑ Creatinine, ↑ BUN:Creatinine ratio, ↑ Urine specific gravity, ↑ Hct IV fluids, thickened liquids if dysphagia, encourage PO intake; assess for aspiration
Hyperglycemia Stress response, reduced activity, medications ↑ Blood glucose (fasting >126, random >200), ↑ HbA1c, ↑ serum glucose Monitor glucose, insulin as needed, dietary management, assess for DM development
🩺 Benztropine + Carbidopa-Levodopa Side Effect Alert
Benztropine SE: Dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, confusion (anticholinergic toxicity)
Carbidopa-Levodopa SE: Dyskinesia, on-off phenomenon, orthostatic hypotension, dark urine/sweat (harmless), hallucinations
Combined: HIGH constipation risk and orthostatic hypotension risk → fall precautions!
🧠 Hemorrhagic Stroke — Case Study Blueprint Priority
Scenario
A 68-year-old hypertensive patient presents with sudden severe headache ("worst headache of my life"), vomiting, nuchal rigidity, and rapid LOC decline. CT scan shows hyperdense area in subarachnoid space.
Pathophysiology Steps
  • Rupture of blood vessel (aneurysm or AVM)
  • Blood accumulates → direct compression of brain tissue
  • ↑ ICP from expanding hematoma
  • Vasospasm → secondary ischemia (3–14 days post-SAH)
  • Cerebral edema worsens → herniation risk
Nursing Assessment / Evaluation
  • Frequent neuro checks (GCS, pupils, motor)
  • Blood pressure management (avoid extreme highs/lows)
  • ICP monitoring (if device in place)
  • CBC, BMP, PT/INR, PTT — coagulation studies
  • CT scan interpretation: blood = bright white (hyperdense)
  • Fall precautions, seizure precautions
⚠ Treatment — What is CONTRAINDICATED and WHY
tPA (thrombolytic) → Dissolves clots → WOULD DRAMATICALLY WORSEN BLEEDING → ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED
Anticoagulants (heparin, warfarin) → Prevent clot formation → WOULD WORSEN BLEEDING → CONTRAINDICATED
Antiplatelets (aspirin, clopidogrel) → Decrease platelet aggregation → WORSEN BLEEDING → CONTRAINDICATED

Treatment instead: Surgical intervention (clipping, coiling), BP control, mannitol for ICP, nimodipine (prevent vasospasm in SAH), supportive care.
