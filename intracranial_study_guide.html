🧠 Intracranial Regulation: The Big Picture

ICR refers to the mechanisms and conditions that impact intracranial processing and function — basically, everything happening inside your skull that keeps your brain working properly.

🔑 Key Concept: The skull is a fixed, rigid space containing three things: brain tissue, blood, and CSF. If one increases, another must decrease — or ICP rises.
🧠 BRAIN + 🩸 BLOOD + 💧 CSF = Constant Pressure
(Monro-Kellie Hypothesis)

Three Things That Can Go Wrong

1. Impaired Perfusion 🩸
The brain needs constant oxygen and blood flow. This depends on cardiac output, blood volume, and hemoglobin's oxygen-carrying capacity. Poor perfusion = neuronal death.
2. Glucose Dysregulation 🍬
Glucose is the brain's primary fuel. Hypoglycemia causes altered mental status. Hyperglycemia worsens brain injury.
3. Neurotransmission Disruption ⚡
Normal brain function requires healthy neurons, intact synapses, and balanced neurotransmitters. Disruptions cause seizures or neurologic dysfunction.

👥 Populations at Risk

🧓 Elderly
Degenerative conditions, falls, and age-related vascular changes
👦 Adolescents/Young Adults
Traumatic brain injury from MVAs, risky behaviors, being struck by objects
🏥 Individual Risk Factors
Hypertension, diabetes, smoking, obesity, cardiovascular disease, drug use (cocaine)

🔬 Cerebral Edema

Fluid buildup around the brain causing increased ICP. It can restrict blood supply and cause irreversible damage — potentially fatal.

Common Causes

Traumatic Brain Injury Ischemic Stroke Brain Abscess Brain Tumor Hemorrhage Meningitis High Altitude

⚡ Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

Normal ICP = 5 – 15 mmHg
CPP = MAP − ICP
🚨 EXAM ALERT — #1 Tested Concept:
The EARLIEST sign of increased ICP is a change in level of consciousness (LOC). Headache is another early sign.

Early Signs of Increased ICP

Change in LOC Headache Restlessness Confusion Pupillary changes

Late Signs — Cushing's Triad ⚠️

Cushing's Triad = Brain Emergency!
Remember: "WIDE, SLOW, IRREGULAR"

🔴 Widened pulse pressure (↑ systolic BP)
🔴 Bradycardia (↓ heart rate)
🔴 Irregular respirations

This is an ominous late sign indicating impending brain herniation!

Other Signs of Increased ICP

Papilledema Impaired eye movement Seizures Vomiting Speech changes Posturing

💊 Treatment for Increased ICP

Prevention
Smoking cessation, healthy weight, BP control, exercise
Pharmacotherapy
Osmotic diuretics (Mannitol), sedatives, analgesics, antiepileptics, glucocorticoids, antipyretics
Surgical
Craniotomy, shunt procedures
Nursing Care
Positioning (HOB 30°), airway management, hyperventilation, bowel/nutrition management, education, rehab

CT scan MRI EEG Skull radiograph Lumbar puncture Brain biopsy

🌩️ Seizures & Epilepsy

Seizure: An abnormal, sudden, excessive, uncontrolled electrical discharge of neurons that may alter consciousness, movement, sensation, or behavior.
Epilepsy: A chronic disorder with recurrent unprovoked seizures, often caused by abnormal electrical neuronal activity or neurotransmitter imbalance.

Causes of Secondary Seizures

Brain tumor Trauma/TBI Metabolic disorders Alcohol withdrawal Electrolyte imbalance High fever Stroke Substance abuse

📊 Phases of a Seizure

This is an NCLEX favorite — know these phases!

1️⃣

Prodrome

tap to reveal

Hours to days before
Early warning signs: headache, malaise, mood changes, altered smell/taste/vision
2️⃣

Aura

tap to reveal

Immediately before seizure
Sensory warning: strange smell, visual disturbance, dizziness, "funny feeling" — can be gustatory, visual, or auditory
3️⃣

Ictal Phase

tap to reveal

The actual seizure event
Includes Tonic Phase (muscle stiffening) and Clonic Phase (rhythmic jerking)
4️⃣

Postictal Phase

tap to reveal

Recovery period after seizure
Confusion, sleepiness, headache, disorientation. Patient may not remember the event.

📋 Types of Seizures

Generalized Seizures
Both hemispheres involved
Types: Tonic-clonic, Clonic, Tonic, Myoclonic, Atonic
Partial (Focal) Seizures
One area of brain
Types: Simple partial, Complex partial
Epilepsy Syndromes
Lennox-Gastaut, Febrile seizures, Pseudoseizures, Status Epilepticus
🚨 Status Epilepticus = Medical emergency! Continuous seizure activity lasting >5 minutes or repeated seizures without recovery between them. Requires immediate intervention.

🩺 Nursing Management of Seizures

✅ Seizure Precautions
• Padded side rails
• Suction at bedside
• Oxygen available
• Bed in lowest position
✅ DURING Seizure — DO:
• Protect head
• Turn patient on side
• Loosen tight clothing
• Note time and duration
❌ DURING Seizure — DO NOT:
• Do NOT restrain patient
• Do NOT put objects in mouth
• Do NOT hold tongue

❤️‍🩹 Stroke Overview

A stroke occurs when blood flow to part of the brain is interrupted, causing tissue death (infarction). It's a medical emergency — sometimes called a "Brain Attack."

🚨 B.E. F.A.S.T. — Know the Signs!

B = Balance (loss, headache, dizziness)
E = Eyes (sudden vision loss)
F = Face (drooping, uneven)
A = Arm (weakness)
S = Speech (difficulty)
T = Time (call 911!)

🔄 Types of Stroke

Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA)

⏱️

tap to reveal

Temporary neurologic dysfunction from brief interruption of blood flow.

• Resolves in 30-60 min (up to 24 hrs)
• Causes: carotid stenosis, atherosclerotic plaque
• Treatment: aspirin, clopidogrel, BP & diabetes control
• "Warning stroke" — take seriously!

Ischemic Stroke

🧊

tap to reveal

Blockage of cerebral artery by thrombus or embolus.

• Thrombotic: atherosclerosis (>50% of strokes)
• Embolic: thrombi break off, travel to brain (often from A-fib, carotid plaques)
• Treatment: tPA within window

Hemorrhagic Stroke

🩸

tap to reveal

Bleeding within brain tissue from ruptured blood vessels.

• Intracerebral: from severe/sustained HTN
• Subarachnoid: ruptured aneurysm or AVM
• Cocaine can trigger sudden BP spike → hemorrhagic stroke
• Aneurysm = abnormal ballooning of artery

🧠 Effects of Stroke by Side

Right Brain Damage →
Paralyzed LEFT side
Spatial/perceptual deficits
Quick, impulsive behavior
Memory deficits (performance)
Left Brain Damage →
Paralyzed RIGHT side
Speech/language deficits
Slow, cautious behavior
Memory deficits (language)

🔍 Stroke Diagnostics & Treatment

CT scan CT angiogram MRI/MRA NIHSS Carotid duplex Lab studies
Treatment Priorities: Maintain airway → Stabilize breathing → Support circulation → Prevent neurological deterioration. Treatment depends on stroke type and extent.

⚡ Broad-Spectrum Antiepileptics

Clonazepam / Diazepam

Anticonvulsant +
RouteClonazepam: PO | Diazepam: IV, IM, PO, PR
UseLennox-Gastaut, akinetic, myoclonic seizures; prevention of acute/abrupt seizures
MechanismAnticonvulsant effects via presynaptic inhibition
Side EffectsPalpitations, rash, nystagmus, suicidal ideation, diplopia
NursingAssess for drowsiness, unsteadiness, clumsiness. Monitor for notable behavioral changes.
ExpectedDecrease/cessation of seizures without undue sedation

Lamotrigine

Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO
UseDecreased seizure incidence; delays mood episodes in bipolar disorder
MechanismStabilizes neuronal membranes by inhibiting sodium transport
Side Effects⚠️ Cardiac arrest, Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS), hepatic failure, blurred vision
NursingMonitor behavioral changes. Monitor for skin changes (SJS risk!).
ExpectedDecrease in frequency or cessation of seizures

Levetiracetam

Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO and IV
UseDecreased incidence and severity of seizures
MechanismInhibits burst firing without affecting normal neuronal excitability
Side EffectsHypertension, SJS, aggression, suicidal ideation
NursingMonitor behavioral changes. Monitor skin changes.
ExpectedDecrease in frequency or cessation of seizures

Topiramate

Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO
UseDecreased incidence of seizures
MechanismBlockade of sodium channels in neurons
Side EffectsSJS, decreased growth in children, vision changes, bleeding, seizures, SI
NursingMonitor behavioral changes. Monitor growth rate in pediatric patients.
ExpectedAbsence or reduction of seizure activity

Valproic Acid

Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO and IV
UseSuppression of seizure activity
MechanismIncreases levels of GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter) in the CNS
Side EffectsPeripheral edema, pancreatitis, hypothermia, rash
NursingAssess seizure activity, assess for SI, monitor for signs of pancreatitis.
ExpectedDecreased seizure activity

🎯 Narrow-Spectrum Antiepileptics

Carbamazepine

Antiepileptic +
RoutePO
UseEpilepsy therapy, seizure prevention, bipolar disorder management
MechanismSuppresses high-frequency neuronal discharge by affecting sodium channels
Side EffectsCNS depression, nystagmus, blurred vision, diplopia, ataxia, vertigo, edema, HF, hypotension, aplastic anemia
NursingMonitor behavioral/skin changes. Monitor lab levels (therapeutic range). Avoid grapefruit!
ExpectedAbsence or reduction of seizure activity

Gabapentin

Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO
UsePartial seizures (adjunct treatment, immediate release only)
Side EffectsSJS, nystagmus, gingivitis, rhabdomyolysis, ataxia, confusion
NursingDo NOT take within 2 hours of antacid. Monitor behavioral/skin changes. Assess seizure activity. Check herbal medicine interactions.
ExpectedDecreased frequency or cessation of seizures

Oxcarbazepine

Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO
UsePartial seizures (monotherapy or adjunctive)
Side EffectsRash, SJS, vision changes, diplopia, nystagmus, NV, dizziness, headache
NursingMonitor behavioral changes, skin changes, and CNS changes.
ExpectedAbsence or reduction of seizure activity

Phenytoin ⭐

HIGH YIELD Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO and IV
UseFocal-onset and generalized seizures; can terminate ventricular arrhythmias
MechanismSuppresses activity of seizure-generating neurons
Side EffectsNystagmus, sedation, ataxia, diplopia, cognitive impairment, gingival hyperplasia, cleft palate to fetus, dysrhythmias, hypotension, Purple Glove Syndrome
NursingMonitor levels (therapeutic range 10-20). GI upset with PO. Monitor enteral feedings (hold tube feeds!).
ExpectedLimit seizure activity
🚨 High-Yield Facts:
• Therapeutic range: 10-20 mcg/mL
• Can cause gingival hyperplasia → teach oral care!
• Purple Glove Syndrome with IV admin
• Teratogenic — cleft palate risk
• Hold tube feeds before/after administration

Pregabalin

Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO
UseDecreased partial-onset seizures
MechanismBinds to calcium channels in CNS tissues regulating neurotransmitter release
Side EffectsPeripheral edema, SJS, vision changes, NVD, dizziness, respiratory depression
NursingMonitor closely for behavioral changes, suicidal thoughts, or depression.
ExpectedDecrease in frequency or cessation of seizures

😴 Sedative/Hypnotic Anticonvulsant

Phenobarbital

Sedative/Anticonvulsant +
RoutePO, IM, IV
UseAnticonvulsant activity
MechanismInhibits transmission in the nervous system and raises seizure threshold
Side EffectsHypotension, rash, constipation, NVD, delirium, depression
NursingMonitor respiratory status, pulse, BP, and signs of angioedema. May take several weeks for maximum anticonvulsant effects.
ExpectedDecrease/cessation of seizures without excessive sedation

💧 Osmotic Diuretic

Mannitol ⭐

HIGH YIELD Osmotic Diuretic +
RouteIV only
UseReduction of intraocular or intracranial pressure
MechanismIncreases osmotic pressure of glomerular filtrate, inhibiting reabsorption of water and electrolytes
Side EffectsHF, chest pain, blurred vision, electrolyte changes, NV, COMA, pulmonary edema
NursingMonitor neuro status and ICP readings. Used to decrease cerebral edema.
ExpectedUrine output ≥ 30-50 mL/hr. Reduction in intracranial pressure.
🚨 Key Point: Monitor urine output! Expected = at least 30-50 mL/hr or an increase per HCP parameters.

🩸 Stroke Medications

Warfarin (Coumadin)

Anticoagulant +
UseStroke prevention, anticoagulation therapy
Key PointsMonitor INR (therapeutic: 2-3). Antidote: Vitamin K. Watch for bleeding. Many drug/food interactions (Vitamin K foods).

Clopidogrel (Plavix)

Antiplatelet +
UsePrevention of thrombotic/embolic strokes, often prescribed post-TIA
Key PointsMonitor for bleeding. Often paired with aspirin after TIA.

tPA (Tissue Plasminogen Activator)

Thrombolytic +
UseTreatment of ischemic stroke — dissolves clots
Key PointsMust be given within treatment window. Major risk: bleeding/hemorrhage. Confirm ischemic (NOT hemorrhagic) stroke before administering!
