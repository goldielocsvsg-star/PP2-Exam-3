Intracranial Regulation (ICR)

Mechanisms & conditions that impact intracranial processing and function — anything affecting the contents of the cranium.

OBJ 1: Risk Factors OBJ 2: Pathophysiology
🧬
What Is ICR?
Core definition
  • ICR = all mechanisms & conditions that impact intracranial processing and function
  • When functioning optimally, ICR allows individuals to function normally
  • Impairment caused by: reduced blood flow, compromised neurotransmitters, or damage to brain tissue
  • Dysfunction ranges from minimal to severe depending on the cause and extent
  • The cranium is a rigid, closed box — any increase in volume inside raises pressure
⚠️
Three Pillars of Impaired ICR
How ICR breaks down
Impaired Perfusion
Brain needs constant O₂ and nutrients via blood. Requires adequate hemoglobin, cardiac function, and lung gas exchange. Think: anemia, heart failure, COPD all impact cerebral perfusion.
Glucose Dysregulation
Carbohydrates are the primary brain fuel. Hypoglycemia is life-threatening — the brain cannot use fat or protein as alternate energy sources effectively.
Altered Neurotransmission
Normal function requires intact neurons, nerves, and balanced neurotransmitters. Imbalance → seizures, altered mental status, psychiatric symptoms.
👥

Increased Intracranial Pressure

Elevated pressure within the rigid skull compresses blood vessels → cerebral hypoxia → brain stem compression → death. Time-critical emergency.

OBJ 2: Pathophysiology OBJ 3: Drug Therapy
📊
ICP Basics — Know the Numbers
  • Normal ICP = 5–15 mmHg — measured by catheter placed in cranium
  • CPP = MAP − ICP — Cerebral Perfusion Pressure; must be maintained ≥50–70 mmHg
  • Increasing ICP → decreasing CPP → brain ischemia
  • The skull is a rigid closed box (Monro-Kellie doctrine): blood + CSF + brain tissue = constant. Increase one → the others must compensate or pressure rises
  • MAP (Mean Arterial Pressure) = DBP + 1/3 (SBP−DBP)
💡
Why we DON'T lower BP aggressively in ICP: If MAP drops, CPP drops → worsening ischemia. Must balance BP management carefully.
🚨
Cushing's Triad — LATE & DEADLY
Indicates impending herniation
  • Wide (widened) pulse pressure — HIGH systolic BP + LOW diastolic = wide gap. Why: brain tries to push more blood against rising pressure.
  • Bradycardia — reflex slowing of heart. Think: CRUSHING triad → CRUSHing HR down.
  • Irregular respirations — Cheyne-Stokes or other abnormal patterns. Brainstem respiratory centers failing.
🚨
Cushing's Triad = EMERGENCY. This is NOT an early sign — by the time Cushing's appears, herniation is imminent. Notify provider IMMEDIATELY.
Memory Trick: CUSHing → CRUSHing: Crushed HR & RR + Wide BP
📈
Progressive Signs of Increased ICP
Early → Moderate → Critical Late

🟢 Early Signs

  • ↓ LOC — irritability, restlessness (EARLIEST sign)
  • Headache
  • Decreased mental status
  • Sleepiness / drowsiness
  • Flat affect, anxiety, confusion

🟡 Moderate Signs

  • Constant headache
  • Sudden vomiting WITHOUT nausea (report to HCP!)
  • Sluggish pupils
  • Unequal pupils
  • Visual changes
  • Lethargy increasing

🔴 Late / DEADLY

  • Fixed & dilated pupils (8mm)
  • Cushing's Triad
  • Cheyne-Stokes respirations
  • Nuchal rigidity (stiff neck)
  • Positive Babinski (adult)
  • Decorticate / Decerebrate posturing
  • Seizures & Coma
🎯
NCLEX PRIORITY: The FIRST/EARLIEST sign of IICP is always a change in LOC — specifically increased irritability, restlessness, or confusion. Vomiting without nausea (projectile vomiting) is a classic moderate sign. Pupil changes signal brainstem compression.
🧍
Abnormal Posturing
Motor responses to severe ICP
Decorticate Posturing (Score 3)
  • Arms FLEX toward core (adducted, flexed against chest)
  • Legs internally rotated, feet turned inward
  • Indicates cortical/cerebral hemisphere damage
  • Bad — but less severe than decerebrate
Decerebrate Posturing (Score 2) — FAR WORSE
  • Arms EXTEND outward (straight, extended, hands curled)
  • Head and neck arched, legs straight, toes pointed downward
  • Indicates brainstem involvement — extremely ominous
  • Memory: DecorEbrate → Extend arms away
🏥
Causes of Increased ICP
Cerebral Edema TBI / Head Trauma Stroke Brain Abscess Brain Tumor Hemorrhage Meningitis High Altitude
Treatment of Increased ICP
  • Mannitol (IV) — osmotic diuretic; pulls fluid from brain tissue → reduces edema
  • Head positioning — HOB 30°, midline, avoid extreme neck flexion
  • Avoid Valsalva — no straining, coughing, sneezing forcefully
  • Controlled hyperventilation (temporary: CO₂↓ → vasoconstriction → ↓ cerebral blood volume)
  • Antiepileptics — prevent seizures which ↑ ICP further
  • Glucocorticoids — reduce edema around tumors/abscesses
  • Surgical: craniotomy, shunt procedures
💊
Nursing Interventions to PREVENT ICP Elevation
Application-level nursing care
  • Quiet, non-stimulating environment — noise, light, pain all ↑ ICP
  • HOB 30° elevation, head midline — promotes venous drainage from brain
  • Avoid extreme hip or neck flexion — compresses jugular veins, traps blood in skull
  • Exhale during repositioning — Valsalva ↑ intrathoracic pressure → ↑ ICP
  • Avoid clustering nursing care — every intervention stimulates and can ↑ ICP
  • O₂ therapy if SpO₂ <95% — hypoxia is a major ICP trigger
  • Stool softeners — prevent straining (Valsalva) during bowel movements
  • Prevent fever — hyperthermia increases metabolic demand → ↑ ICP
  • Do NOT put in Trendelenburg (head-down) — increases cerebral blood volume
  • Monitor ICP readings — report values >15 mmHg or trends of increase
🚫
CONTRAINDICATED with IICP: Blowing nose, isometric exercises (push against resistance), vigorous coughing, Trendelenburg position, oversedation without airway protection, nasal packing (if CSF leak). These all raise intrathoracic or venous pressure.
⚠️
LUMBAR PUNCTURE — Contraindicated if IICP suspected: LP must be DEFERRED until AFTER a head CT when intracranial hypertension is suspected. Performing an LP with elevated ICP can precipitate brainstem herniation as pressure suddenly shifts. Always image first. CT → then LP only if safe and needed (e.g., rule out meningitis, confirm SAH when CT is negative).

Seizures & Epilepsy

Abnormal, sudden, excessive, uncontrolled electrical discharge of neurons — can cause LOC changes, motor/sensory changes, or behavioral changes.

OBJ 2: PathophysiologyOBJ 3: Drug Therapy
⚡
Seizure vs. Epilepsy
  • Seizure: Single episode of abnormal electrical neuronal discharge. May be provoked (fever, electrolytes, alcohol withdrawal) or unprovoked
  • Epilepsy: Chronic disorder with REPEATED, UNPROVOKED seizures. Caused by imbalance of neurotransmitters (↑ excitatory or ↓ inhibitory)
  • Status Epilepticus: Continuous or recurring seizure activity lasting ≥30 minutes — a medical emergency! Risk of permanent brain damage, hypoxia, hyperthermia
🎯
Key distinction: A single febrile seizure ≠ epilepsy. Epilepsy = REPEATED + UNPROVOKED seizures.
🔬
Etiology of Seizures

Primary/Idiopathic

  • No identifiable brain lesion
  • Genetic factors likely involved
  • No specific cause found

Secondary Causes

  • Brain tumor/trauma
  • Metabolic disorders
  • Electrolyte disturbances (hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia)
  • Alcohol withdrawal
  • High fever
  • Stroke/head injury
  • Substance abuse

Seizure Phases — Know the Timeline
Prodrome Phase
Early clinical manifestations occurring DAYS TO HOURS before seizure. Headache, malaise, changes in smell/taste, hearing/vision. Patient may sense something is coming.
Aura Phase
Sensation that immediately precedes seizure onset. Gustatory (taste), visual (lights/colors), or auditory (sounds). Some experience dizziness, numbness, or "funny feeling." Aura = warning to get safe.
Ictal Phase
The actual seizure event. Includes tonic phase (muscle rigidity, apnea possible) and clonic phase (rhythmic jerking). Duration varies by type.
Tonic Phase
State of muscle contraction with excessive muscle tone. Stiffening. May have apnea → cyanosis possible. Lasts seconds to ~1 minute.
Clonic Phase
State of alternating contraction and relaxation of muscles. Rhythmic jerking movements. Gradually slowing.
Postictal Phase
Period immediately following cessation of seizure activity. Patient confused, exhausted, may not remember seizure. May last minutes to hours. Key nursing intervention: turn to side, assess LOC, protect airway.
🛡️
Seizure Precautions
Set up BEFORE a seizure occurs
  • Pad side rails of bed — prevent injury during jerking
  • Place airway at bedside — oral airway for post-seizure positioning
  • Oxygen and suction at bedside — maintain airway post-ictally
  • IV access patent — flush regularly; medications given IV in emergency
  • Bed in LOW position — NOT high! Fall prevention
  • Do NOT put padded tongue blade — never place anything in mouth!
  • Remove sharp objects from environment
🚫
NEVER: Restrain limbs, force objects into mouth, put fingers in mouth during seizure. These cause injury, not safety.
🏃
Nursing Care DURING & AFTER Seizure
  • Time the seizure — duration is critical clinical data
  • Do NOT restrain — guide limbs gently if needed
  • Loosen restrictive clothing
  • Position to side (lateral) if possible — prevents aspiration
  • Head flexed forward — maintains airway
  • Raise padded side rails
  • After seizure: assess LOC, breathing, orientation
  • Suction if needed; apply O₂
  • Document: what happened before, during, after; body parts involved; duration
  • If seizure >5 min or no recovery between seizures → administer emergency anticonvulsant
🎯
NCLEX Application — Status Epilepticus: An intubated patient seizing for 20+ minutes = Status Epilepticus. First-line drug = Lorazepam IV (NOT diazepam IV first if intubated, though diazepam is also used). Phenobarbital is used when benzodiazepines fail. For children with status epilepticus = often Diazepam IV/PR, then Phenobarbital if no response.
⚡
Common Seizure Triggers
Factors that lower seizure threshold
  • Hypoglycemia — brain deprived of fuel; major trigger
  • Sleep deprivation / fatigue — lowers neuronal threshold
  • Fever / infection — hyperthermia increases neuronal excitability
  • Emotional or physical stress — heightens CNS excitation
  • Alcohol withdrawal — rebound CNS excitation after chronic suppression
  • Electrolyte imbalances — hyperkalemia, hyponatremia (large water intake), hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia
  • Flashing/blinking lights — photosensitive epilepsy trigger
  • Missed AED doses — most common cause of breakthrough seizures; ~50% of treatment failures
  • Abrupt AED withdrawal — can precipitate status epilepticus
  • Certain medications — antipsychotics (especially with alcohol), hyperventilation
🔬
Seizure Diagnostics & AED Rules
Workup + key medication principles
Diagnostic Workup
  • EEG (Electroencephalogram) — gold standard; measures electrical activity; video-EEG localizes seizure origin; confirms vs. pseudoseizures
  • CT scan — detects structural causes (tumors, hemorrhage, trauma)
  • MRI — best soft tissue visualization for seizure focus
  • Labs — blood glucose, serum calcium, electrolytes (rule out metabolic triggers)
  • Lumbar puncture — if infection (meningitis) suspected as seizure cause
AED Key Rules
  • NEVER stop AEDs abruptly — abrupt withdrawal → seizures or status epilepticus
  • Taper over 6 weeks to several months when discontinuing; if on two drugs, withdraw one at a time
  • Strict adherence is critical — missing doses is the #1 cause of treatment failure
  • Monitor therapeutic blood levels — especially phenytoin (10–20 mcg/mL); helps detect toxicity and nonadherence
  • Do not drive or do hazardous activities until seizure control is confirmed
  • All AEDs carry suicide risk — monitor for mood changes, agitation, depression, suicidal ideation

Stroke — Brain Attack

Interruption of perfusion to any part of the brain, resulting in infarction. Time-critical: every minute counts — 1.9 million neurons die per minute without treatment.

Hemorrhagic Stroke
  • Intracerebral hemorrhage — bleeding INTO brain tissue; most commonly from severe/sustained hypertension
  • Elevated BP → arterial wall changes → rupture → blood in brain tissue compresses/destroys neurons
  • Cocaine use can trigger sudden dramatic BP elevation → hemorrhagic stroke
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage — bleeding into subarachnoid space; typically from ruptured aneurysm or AVM
  • Aneurysm = abnormal ballooning or blister along a normal artery
⚠️
tPA is CONTRAINDICATED in hemorrhagic stroke! You must differentiate stroke type via CT BEFORE administering thrombolytics.
🫀
Ischemic Stroke
  • Thrombotic — most common (>50% of all strokes); associated with atherosclerosis and hypercoagulation; plaque builds up in cerebral or carotid arteries → occlusion
  • Embolic — thrombus or clot breaks off from elsewhere (typically cardiac source = atrial fibrillation, or carotid artery plaques) → travels to cerebral arteries
  • Both result in brain ischemia and infarction within minutes
  • tPA is the treatment if given within the therapeutic window
tPA Window3–4.5 hours from symptom onset (standard). Must rule out hemorrhagic stroke first via CT.
TIATemporary neurologic dysfunction; resolves within 30–60 min (up to 24h); precursor to stroke — act now
🧠
Left vs. Right Brain Stroke Effects
Brain damage affects OPPOSITE side of body (contralateral)

🧠 Left Brain Stroke

  • Right-sided paralysis and sensory changes
  • Speech/language problems — aphasia, dysarthria
  • Abstract thinking deficits
  • Memory problems (language-based)
  • Slow, cautious behavioral style
  • Difficulty reading, writing, calculating

🧠 Right Brain Stroke

  • Left-sided paralysis and sensory changes
  • Spatial/perceptual deficits
  • Left-sided neglect (ignores left side)
  • Memory problems (performance-based)
  • Quick, impulsive behavioral style
  • Poor judgment, overestimates abilities
🎯
NCLEX Tip: Left brain stroke → RIGHT-sided deficits + speech problems. Right brain stroke → LEFT-sided deficits + spatial/perceptual problems. The behavioral style difference is also tested: Left = slow/cautious; Right = quick/impulsive/poor safety judgment.
💊
Stroke Pharmacological Management
  • tPA (Alteplase) — thrombolytic; breaks down clot in ischemic stroke; ≤3–4.5h window; monitor for bleeding complications
  • Anticoagulants — Warfarin; prevention of embolic strokes
  • Antiplatelets — Clopidogrel, Aspirin; post-TIA, post-ischemic stroke prevention
  • BP medications — ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers; control hypertension (primary stroke risk)
  • Statins — atorvastatin (Lipitor), pravastatin, rosuvastatin (Crestor), simvastatin (Zocor); reduce atherosclerosis/plaque
  • Nimodipine — calcium channel blocker; used to treat/prevent cerebral vasospasm after subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH); relaxes cerebral vessel smooth muscle to improve distal perfusion
  • Stool softeners — prevent straining/Valsalva which raises ICP; important in all neurologic patients
🏥
Stroke Nursing Priorities
  • Airway first — ensure respiratory status, cardiovascular function stable; ABCs before anything else
  • Rapid CT scan (non-contrast) — rule out hemorrhagic stroke BEFORE any thrombolytics; CT is the FIRST test
  • NIHSS score — establish baseline neurological deficit; use standardized scale consistently
  • tPA (Alteplase) requirements: BP must be <185/110 mmHg BEFORE administration; dose 0.9 mg/kg (10% over first minute, rest over 60 min); goal door-to-needle (DTN) ≤45 min
  • Post-tPA monitoring: Maintain BP <180/105; frequent neuro checks; hold infusion for severe headache, vomiting, or sudden BP spike (may indicate hemorrhagic conversion); avoid invasive lines for 24h
  • Dysphagia assessment FIRST — patient is NPO until swallowing is formally screened; SLP consult as needed; aspiration is a major early complication
  • Do NOT lower BP too aggressively in ischemic stroke — high BP may be maintaining perfusion to ischemic penumbra; lowering too fast can worsen ischemia
  • Prevent ICP elevation — HOB 30°, head midline, avoid clustering care, O₂ if SpO₂ <95%, quiet environment
  • Communication adaptations — aphasia is common; see aphasia guide below; never assume patient cannot understand
  • Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin, clopidogrel) — post-ischemic stroke and TIA; reduce platelet stickiness to prevent recurrent thrombosis; NOT given within 24h of tPA
💬
Aphasia vs. Dysarthria
Communication deficits after stroke
Expressive Aphasia (Broca's)
  • Understands language but CANNOT express it verbally or in writing
  • Speech is non-fluent; knows what they want to say but can't get the words out
  • Often very frustrated — they understand everything
  • Nursing: Ask yes/no questions; use simple phrases; allow extra time; do NOT speak loudly
Receptive Aphasia (Wernicke's)
  • Cannot understand spoken or written language
  • Speech may be fluent but meaningless (word salad)
  • Nursing: Use gestures, pictures, and cues; speak slowly; use one-step commands
Dysarthria
  • Motor speech disorder — slurred, slow, or poorly articulated speech from muscle weakness
  • Language understanding is INTACT — patient knows what to say, just can't articulate it clearly
  • Different from aphasia — aphasia is a language problem; dysarthria is a motor/muscle problem
👁
SAH Classic Presentation & Neglect
Key clinical patterns
Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH) — Red Flag
  • "Worst headache of my life" — thunderclap headache; sudden, explosive onset within seconds
  • Nausea, vomiting, photophobia, neck stiffness (meningeal irritation)
  • Decreased LOC, cranial nerve deficits, possible seizures
  • Diagnostic sequence: Non-contrast CT first → if negative but SAH still suspected → Lumbar puncture (look for xanthochromia)
  • Vasospasm risk — occurs 3–14 days post-SAH; causes delayed cerebral ischemia and new neuro deficits; managed with Nimodipine (calcium channel blocker — relaxes cerebral vessel spasm)
Left-Sided Neglect (Right Brain Stroke)
  • Patient ignores or is unaware of the left side of their body and environment
  • Nursing: Approach from the UNAFFECTED (right) side initially; teach patient to scan to the left; place call light and meals on the right; dress affected (left) side first
  • Safety risk — patient may bump into objects on left side; fall precautions essential
  • Homonymous hemianopsia — loss of same visual field in both eyes; teach head-turning and scanning to compensate
📚
Stroke Diagnostics
CT Scan — First-line (rule out hemorrhage) CT Angiogram — Visualize cerebral vessels MRI / MRA — Best soft tissue visualization Carotid Duplex Studies — Carotid stenosis NIHSS — Stroke severity Labs: CBC, CMP, PT/INR, PTT EKG — Rule out AFib source Lumbar Puncture — If SAH suspected with negative CT

🔵 BROAD SPECTRUM ANTIEPILEPTICS (AEDs)
Diazepam / Lorazepam
(Valium / Ativan) · Benzodiazepine / Antiseizure
Benzodiazepine
MOA
  • Presynaptic inhibition via GABA-A receptor enhancement
  • ↑ chloride influx → neuronal hyperpolarization → suppresses seizures
Therapeutic Use
  • Lennox-Gastaut, akinetic, myoclonic seizures
  • Prevention of acute/abrupt seizures
  • Lorazepam IV = FIRST LINE for Status Epilepticus
  • Diazepam: IV, IM, PO, PR, nasal routes
Adverse Effects
  • Bradypnea, bradycardia
  • Respiratory depression (major risk!)
  • Rapid IV admin → apnea, cardiac arrest
  • Palpitations, nystagmus, diplopia
  • Rash, drowsiness, unsteadiness
Nursing Interventions
  • Assess for drowsiness, unsteadiness, clumsiness
  • Monitor closely for behavioral changes
  • Have resuscitation equipment available
  • Do NOT give rapid IV push
  • Monitor respiratory status continuously
Evaluation / Expected Response
  • Decrease or cessation of seizure activity WITHOUT undue sedation
Valproic Acid
(Depakote, Depakene) · Antiseizure · PO and IV
Broad Spectrum AED
MOA
  • Increases levels of GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter) in the CNS
  • ↑ GABA → ↓ neuronal excitability
Use
  • Suppression of seizure activity (broad spectrum)
  • Bipolar disorder, migraine prevention
Adverse Effects
  • Hepatotoxicity — liver failure possible
  • Pancreatitis
  • Peripheral edema, hypothermia
  • Blood dyscrasias (leukopenia, thrombocytopenia)
  • Teratogen — neural tube defects
Nursing / Monitoring
  • CONTRAINDICATED in LIVER DISEASE
  • Assess for jaundice, abdominal pain, N/V (hepatic failure signs)
  • Monitor for pancreatitis: abdominal pain, N/V, anorexia
  • Monitor CBC for blood dyscrasias
  • Assess seizure activity and for rash
Patient Teaching
  • Do NOT stop abruptly — seizure risk
  • Report signs of liver problems immediately (yellow skin/eyes, dark urine)
  • Avoid alcohol — increased CNS depression
  • Women of childbearing age: highly teratogenic
Levetiracetam
(Keppra) · Antiseizure · PO and IV
Broad Spectrum AED
MOA
  • Inhibits burst firing without affecting normal neuronal excitability
  • Does NOT affect normal neuronal function — selective for hyperactive seizure-generating neurons
Use
  • Decreased incidence and severity of seizures
  • Adjunctive therapy for partial-onset seizures
Adverse Effects
  • Most common: drowsiness, weakness
  • Hypertension
  • Agitation, behavioral changes
  • SJS (rare)
  • Bone marrow suppression (rare)
Nursing
  • Monitor for behavioral changes (mood, hostility, agitation)
  • Monitor for skin changes (SJS risk)
  • Assess seizure frequency
Lamotrigine
(Lamictal) · Antiseizure · PO
Broad Spectrum AED
MOA
  • Stabilizes neuronal membranes by inhibiting sodium transport
  • Reduces pre-synaptic neurotransmitter release
Use
  • Decreased incidence of seizures
  • Bipolar disorder (delayed time to mood episode recurrence)
Adverse Effects
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) — life-threatening rash
  • Cardiac arrest
  • Hepatic failure
  • Blurred vision
  • Increased suicidal ideation — higher risk than other AEDs
Nursing
  • Monitor for suicidal ideation (higher risk than other antiseizures)
  • Monitor for skin changes — report any rash immediately
  • Monitor behavioral changes
  • Rash development = stop drug, contact HCP
Topiramate
(Topamax) · Antiseizure · PO
Broad Spectrum AED
MOA
  • Blockade of sodium channels in neurons
  • Enhances GABA activity
Adverse Effects
  • Common: somnolence, dizziness, ataxia, nervousness, anorexia, weight loss
  • Rare: SJS, decreased growth in children
  • Vision changes: ocular pain, sudden worsening or blurred vision (report immediately — acute glaucoma)
  • Hyperthermia
Nursing
  • Monitor behavioral changes
  • Monitor growth rate in children
  • Any visual changes/eye pain → report immediately
  • Monitor for signs of hyperthermia
🟡 NARROW SPECTRUM ANTIEPILEPTICS
Phenytoin
(Dilantin) · Anticonvulsant/Antiepileptic · PO and IV
Narrow Spectrum
MOA
  • Suppresses activity of seizure-generating neurons
  • Stabilizes neuronal membranes — blocks Na⁺ channels
  • Also terminates ventricular arrhythmias
Use
  • Focal-onset and generalized-onset seizures
  • Status epilepticus (IV)
  • Ventricular arrhythmias
Adverse Effects — HIGH YIELD
  • Gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth)
  • Purple glove syndrome (IV extravasation)
  • Nystagmus, sedation, ataxia, diplopia
  • Cognitive impairment
  • Teratogen — cleft palate in fetus
  • Decreases effectiveness of oral contraceptives
  • Dysrhythmias and hypotension (IV)
  • Measles-like rash → risk of SJS
Nursing
  • Therapeutic Range: 10–20 mcg/mL — monitor levels
  • Monitor enteral feedings: hold 1 hour before AND 1 hour after (absorption interference)
  • Avoid grapefruit juice
  • Women of childbearing age: needs reliable NON-oral contraception
  • If pregnant, consult provider first
  • GI upset with PO route — take with food
Patient Teaching
  • Excellent oral hygiene — daily brushing and flossing (prevent gingival hyperplasia)
  • Report any rash to HCP immediately
  • Do NOT consume alcohol — increased CNS depression
  • Keep ALL follow-up appointments for medication level monitoring
  • Avoid grapefruit and grapefruit juice
Carbamazepine
(Tegretol) · Antiepileptic · PO
Narrow Spectrum
MOA
  • Decreases synaptic transmission by affecting Na⁺ channels
  • Suppresses high-frequency neuronal discharge in seizure foci
Use
  • Epilepsy — focal and generalized seizures
  • Bipolar disorder
  • Trigeminal neuralgia
Adverse Effects
  • CNS depressant effects, visual disturbances (nystagmus, blurred vision, diplopia)
  • Ataxia, vertigo, headache
  • Aplastic anemia (monitor CBC)
  • Fluid retention → edema (caution in heart failure)
  • SJS risk
Nursing
  • Monitor lab levels for therapeutic range
  • AVOID GRAPEFRUIT (enzyme interaction — increases carbamazepine levels → toxicity)
  • Do NOT take within 2 hours of antacid (antacid decreases absorption)
  • Monitor skin changes, behavioral changes (suicidal ideation risk)
  • Assess for fluid retention in cardiac patients
  • Monitor CBC — aplastic anemia risk
Gabapentin
(Neurontin) · Anticonvulsant · PO
Narrow Spectrum
Adverse Effects
  • SJS, nystagmus, gingivitis
  • Ataxia, confusion
  • Rhabdomyolysis (rare)
Nursing

Oxcarbazepine
(Trileptal) · Anticonvulsant · PO
Narrow Spectrum
MOA
  • Blocks voltage-sensitive sodium channels
  • Stabilizes hyperexcitable neuronal membranes → prevents seizure spread
Use
  • Partial (focal) seizures — monotherapy or adjunctive therapy
  • Adults and children
Adverse Effects
  • CNS: dizziness, drowsiness, diplopia, nystagmus, headache, ataxia
  • Hyponatremia (sodium <125 mmol/L) — nausea, headache, confusion, drowsiness
  • SJS/TEN — especially in patients with HLA-B*1502 (Asian descent)
  • Hypothyroidism (especially in children)
  • Rash, vision changes, GI upset
Nursing
  • Monitor sodium levels — hyponatremia is a key risk
  • Monitor thyroid function (TSH) — hypothyroidism risk
  • Monitor for skin changes — SJS risk; consider HLA-B*1502 testing in Asian patients
  • Monitor for behavioral changes and suicidal ideation
  • Teach alternative contraception — decreases oral contraceptive effectiveness
  • Taper slowly when discontinuing (1–6 months)
Expected Response: Absence or reduction of seizure activity without excessive sedation.

Mannitol
Osmotic Diuretic · IV ONLY
Osmotic Diuretic
MOA
  • Increases the osmotic pressure of glomerular filtrate
  • Inhibits reabsorption of water and electrolytes
  • Draws fluid OUT of brain tissue (cerebral edema) into bloodstream → excreted via kidneys
Use
  • Reduction of INTRACRANIAL pressure (cerebral edema)
  • Reduction of intraocular pressure
  • Forced diuresis
Adverse Effects
  • Heart failure / pulmonary edema (fluid shifts)
  • Chest pain
  • Blurred vision, electrolyte imbalances
  • N/V
  • COMA (if over-diuresis occurs)
Nursing
  • Monitor neurologic status and ICP readings
  • Monitor urine output: goal ≥30–50 mL/hr
  • Monitor electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺)
  • Use filter needle for IV administration
  • Assess for signs of fluid overload (crackles, peripheral edema)
Evaluation
  • Urine output ≥30–50 mL/hr (or per HCP parameters)
  • Reduction in intracranial pressure
  • Improved neurological status
